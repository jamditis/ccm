The big picture

As local newsrooms across New Jersey continue to shrink, residents increasingly turn to alternative sources for information about their communities. Our research suggests that social media influencers — content creators with dedicated local followings — are filling some of these gaps, whether intentionally or not.

This doesn't mean influencers are replacing journalists. They lack the institutional accountability, editorial oversight, and professional training that define journalism. But it does mean that audiences are finding local information through channels we haven't fully studied — and that has implications for how we think about local news and information.

We wanted to understand: how much local information are NJ influencers actually providing? What topics do they cover? And what does their content look like compared to traditional news?

What this means

While lifestyle content dominates — expected given the nature of influencer platforms — the combined news, opinion, and educational categories represent a meaningful information-sharing function. When we expand the definition to include posts that use an "informative" rhetorical approach (explaining, teaching, or reporting rather than purely entertaining), nearly half of all content serves some informational purpose.

This suggests many NJ influencers are doing more than entertainment. They're explaining local issues, sharing opinions on community matters, and educating audiences about topics that affect their daily lives — even when their primary identity isn't "news creator."

Intentionally local content

A creator living in New Jersey could make content about anything — national trends, generic lifestyle tips, universal entertainment. The high local relevance scores we found suggest something different: these influencers are consciously creating content about and for New Jersey communities.

They're talking about specific restaurants in Hoboken, covering high school basketball games in Essex County, reviewing new developments in Jersey City. This hyperlocal focus is precisely the kind of coverage that traditional news outlets have reduced as resources have shrunk.

06

What we can conclude

NJ influencers provide meaningful local information. Nearly 1 in 5 posts contain news, opinion, or educational content. When we include posts with informative intent, that rises to almost half. These creators are doing more than entertainment — they're explaining, teaching, and reporting on local matters.

They're filling coverage gaps. High school sports, local restaurants, neighborhood developments, community events — these are beats that traditional newsrooms have largely abandoned due to resource constraints. Influencers are covering them, often with significant engagement from local audiences.

The content is genuinely local. More than half of all posts scored high on NJ relevance. These creators are making content specifically about and for New Jersey communities, not generic content that happens to be made by someone in NJ.

The tone is positive but informative. Unlike the confrontational tone often associated with social media, NJ influencer content skews positive and uses informative rhetorical approaches. Joy and anticipation are the dominant emotions.

Implications for local journalism

News organizations might consider partnerships with trusted local influencers for specific coverage areas. Influencer networks could help newsrooms reach audiences who don't consume traditional news. And studying what makes influencer content engaging could inform how newsrooms approach local coverage.

None of this replaces the accountability function of professional journalism. But understanding where audiences actually get local information — and who's providing it — matters for anyone working to strengthen local news.

07

How we did this

Data collection

We identified 41 New Jersey-based influencers through purposive sampling, selecting creators who maintain active presence on TikTok, Instagram, or YouTube; produce content with explicit NJ focus; and have established local followings. Content was collected in November-December 2025 using platform-specific tools: yt-dlp for TikTok and YouTube, Instaloader for Instagram. For video content, we transcribed audio using OpenAI Whisper to enable text-based analysis.

Visual content extraction

Many social media videos contain on-screen text (captions, titles, graphics) that isn't captured in auto-generated subtitles or audio transcription. To address this, we developed a multi-stage visual extraction process:

  1. Frame extraction: We extracted one screenshot every 2 seconds from each video, creating a comprehensive visual timeline of the content.
  2. Grid composition: Screenshots were grouped into composite grids, allowing efficient batch processing while preserving temporal relationships within the video.
  3. OCR analysis: We applied optical character recognition to these grids to capture on-screen text overlays, branded graphics, location tags, and other visual text elements.
  4. Change detection: Screenshots were clustered to identify visual transitions and transformations over time, capturing moments where new information appeared on screen.

This multi-modal approach ensured we captured the full textual content of videos, including creator-added captions, text overlays, and graphics that are common in TikTok and Instagram Reels content.

AI analysis approach

We used large language models for scalable content classification, running two types of analysis:

Semantic analysis classified each post's main topic, content type (news, lifestyle, entertainment, promotion, opinion, educational), NJ relevance score (0-1), local vs. universal appeal, key messages, target audience, and entities mentioned.

Sentiment analysis measured sentiment score (-1 to +1), primary emotion, rhetorical mode (informative, entertaining, persuasive, confrontational, inspirational), and authenticity assessment.

How we defined key metrics

NJ relevance score (0-1): AI models assessed how specifically content relates to New Jersey places, people, issues, or events. Scores 0.0-0.3 = no NJ elements; 0.4-0.6 = indirect connections; 0.7-1.0 = content explicitly about and for NJ audiences. Posts scoring ≥0.7 were classified as "high NJ relevance."

News-adjacent content (16.7%): Posts classified as news (current events, public affairs, community developments), opinion (commentary on local issues), or educational (instructional content, history, explainers). This distinguishes informational content from lifestyle and entertainment.

Informative rhetorical mode (43.1%): Separate from content type — a restaurant review is "lifestyle" content but uses an "informative" mode. This captures content where the primary intent is to explain, teach, or convey information, even when not explicitly "news."

Why batch processing?

We initially ran analysis using real-time API calls, but switched to batch processing for several reasons:

  • 50% cost savings — All three providers offer batch APIs with half-price rates for async processing
  • Higher throughput — No rate limiting concerns; we could submit all 3,650 posts at once
  • Reliability — Batch APIs handle retries automatically and don't time out on long-running jobs

Trade-off: Batch results take longer (typically under 1 hour, up to 24 hours max). For research purposes where we don't need real-time results, batch processing is clearly the better choice. Total analysis cost was approximately $15-20 for 3,650 posts.

Technical implementation

The full analysis pipeline was built in Python and consists of several stages. All code is available for research transparency.

1. Data collection (scraping)

Platform-specific scrapers download up to 50 recent posts per influencer:

  • TikTok + YouTube: yt-dlp — downloads videos, thumbnails, and metadata JSON
  • Instagram: instaloader — downloads posts with captions, engagement metrics, and metadata

Key scripts: main.py orchestrates collection; scrapers/tiktok.py, scrapers/instagram.py, scrapers/youtube.py handle platform-specific logic.

2. Video processing

Video content requires additional processing before text analysis:

  • Audio extraction: FFmpeg extracts audio tracks from video files
  • Transcription: OpenAI Whisper (local deployment) converts speech to text with timestamps
  • OCR: Frame extraction + optical character recognition captures on-screen text (captions, titles, text overlays)

Key scripts: analysis/video_processor/transcriber.py, ocr_processor.py, audio_extractor.py

3. Data consolidation

Platform-specific parsers normalize metadata into a unified format:

  • Each platform has a parser (tiktok_parser.py, youtube_parser.py, instagram_parser.py) that extracts relevant fields
  • consolidate.py merges all posts into all_posts.csv with ~40 columns
  • Calculates per-influencer aggregate metrics (engagement, views, post counts)

Output: analysis/data/all_posts.csv — 3,650 rows with video_id, influencer_name, platform, title, description, transcript, ocr_text, engagement metrics, timestamps, etc.

4. AI content analysis

Two analysis passes run on each post:

  • Semantic analysis (semantic_analyzer.py): Extracts topic, content type, NJ relevance score, entities, target audience
  • Sentiment analysis (sentiment_analyzer.py): Measures emotional valence, primary emotions, rhetorical mode, authenticity

Both analyzers use structured JSON prompts and validate responses against expected schemas. Checkpointing saves progress every 10 posts so analysis can resume after interruption.

5. Multi-provider orchestration

To manage costs and throughput, we distributed analysis across three providers:

  • Claude batch: batch_analyzer.py uses Anthropic's Message Batches API — submit all requests at once, poll for results
  • Gemini batch: Google's batch API with similar async processing
  • OpenAI: Standard synchronous API with rate limiting and retry logic

Results from all providers are merged into unified JSON files with consistent schema. Provider metadata is preserved for analysis.

6. Visualization generation

generate_visualizations.py creates publication-quality charts using matplotlib and seaborn:

  • 10 figures total: sentiment distribution, emotion breakdown, rhetorical modes, NJ relevance histogram, content types pie chart, topic analysis, influencer comparisons, summary dashboard
  • Output: analysis/figures/ directory with PNG files

Limitations

Sample size: 41 influencers is a starting point, not a full survey of NJ creators. Results may not generalize to all NJ influencers.

Platform coverage: We analyzed TikTok, Instagram, and YouTube only. This excludes X/Twitter, Facebook, Substack, newsletters, podcasts, and other channels where influencers may share different types of content.

Temporal scope: Content was collected in late 2025. Influencer practices, platform algorithms, and content trends evolve — these findings represent a snapshot, not a longitudinal view.

AI classification reliability: LLM-based analysis has inherent limitations. Models can misclassify content, especially when posts have limited text or ambiguous intent. Some posts lacked sufficient content for confident classification.

No audience perspective: This study examines content production, not how audiences perceive or use this content. We don't know if viewers trust influencer information, seek it out deliberately, or act on it.

Selection bias: Our purposive sampling may have selected influencers who are more information-oriented than average. A random sample might show different results.
