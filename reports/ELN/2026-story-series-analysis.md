## ANALYSIS: WHY STORY SERIES MATTER

### Impact Multiplier Effect
Individual stories create awareness. **Series create change.** The Montclair schools deficit coverage didn't just report a problem—it forced official accountability, legal intervention, and policy changes. The Delbarton trial coverage didn't just cover a verdict—it created historical record and institutional accountability.

### Sustained Accountability
One-off stories let officials wait out the news cycle. Series coverage shows reporters won't move on until issues are resolved. This sustained pressure is essential for democracy.

### Complexity Mastered
Major issues like school budgets, corruption cases, and immigration policy are too complex for single stories. Series allow reporters to build public understanding over time, making arcane topics accessible.

### Community Trust
Series coverage shows outlets are invested in communities long-term, not just cherry-picking sensational stories. This builds trust and source relationships that enable better future reporting.

### Resource Commitment
Series require significant resource allocation—time, reporter expertise, editor support. Recognizing series rewards outlets that make this investment despite financial pressures.

## METHODOLOGY NOTES

This analysis searched the Story Submissions CSV for:
- Multiple stories from same outlet on same topic
- Stories published over weeks/months showing sustained coverage
- Related stories from different outlets on same major issue
- Headlines indicating ongoing investigations or developing stories
- Topics matching categories identified: schools, corruption, immigration, abuse, environment

The analysis prioritized:
- Length of sustained coverage
- Number of distinct stories
- Multiple angles/approaches to same issue
- Documented impact on policy or accountability
- Quality and depth of individual pieces
- Resource commitment required

---

## RECOMMENDATIONS FOR JUDGES

When evaluating story series vs. individual stories:

1. **Consider cumulative impact** - Did the series create change that individual stories couldn't?
2. **Assess resource commitment** - How many months? How many reporters? How much depth?
3. **Evaluate completion** - Did outlet follow through to resolution, or abandon mid-story?
4. **Measure complexity** - Did series help public understand difficult issues?
5. **Check accountability** - Did sustained coverage force institutional response?
6. **Value persistence** - Did reporters resist pressure to move on?
7. **Recognize innovation** - Did series use creative approaches (calculators, multi-part narratives)?

Series journalism is increasingly rare as outlets cut staff and chase clicks. Recognizing excellent series work encourages outlets to maintain this essential democratic function.
